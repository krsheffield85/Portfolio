Vibe Coding: When AI Writes the Code

Source: Elektor Magazine (see article link)

Article link

← Back to Weekly Articles

Summary

Main Findings

The article explains how the rapid rise of AI-assisted programming in 2025 led to the widespread adoption of a development style known as “vibe coding.” In this workflow, developers describe what they want in natural language, accept the code generated by AI models, and repeatedly refine it by responding to errors or adjusting prompts. While this method allowed developers to produce working software quickly, many users did not fully understand the code they were deploying.

The term “vibe coding” was popularized by AI researcher Andrej Karpathy and describes a style of programming where intuition and rapid iteration drive development instead of formal engineering discipline. The article argues that vibe coding can be effective for prototypes, personal projects, and simple applications, but it introduces risk when used for complex or mission-critical systems.

A key issue raised is that AI-generated code can create a false sense of confidence. Developers may accept outputs that work in the short term but contain hidden security problems, poor architecture, or maintenance issues. The article notes that debugging and long-term support can become harder when people are building systems they do not fully understand.

Broader Implications for Society

Vibe coding lowers the barrier to entry for software creation, which could expand innovation and productivity. At the same time, it could also increase the spread of insecure or poorly tested software. As AI-generated coding becomes normal, the skill gap may widen between people who can critically evaluate AI outputs and those who rely on them blindly.

Practical Takeaway

The article concludes that vibe coding is a powerful tool, but it should be paired with real engineering practices: code review, testing, documentation, and security checks. Without those guardrails, the speed gains can be offset by long-term technical debt and risk.
